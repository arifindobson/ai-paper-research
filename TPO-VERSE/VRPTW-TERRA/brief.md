# A Heterogeneous Hybrid Routing Framework

**Adapting Spatial Indexing (Uber H3) and Open-Source Optimization (VROOM/OSM) across Asymmetric Indonesian Topographies**

---

## 1. Core Problem Statement

A monolithic routing engine fails in Indonesia due to regional divergence. This paper introduces a **Context-Aware Parameterization Layer** to the VROOM/OSM/H3 stack, evaluating performance across two diametrically opposed archetypes:

- **The Hyper-Dense Megacity (e.g., Jakarta):** extreme node density, severe traffic penalties, and highly restrictive time-windows.
- **The Archipelagic/Isolated Hub (e.g., Ambon):** low node density, linear coastal routes, topographic barriers, and high-stake maritime/inter-island dependencies.

## 2. Adapting the Tech Stack to Indonesian Diversity

To make the engine resilient to these differences, each component must behave differently based on the target region:

| Component | Archetype A: Jakarta (Megacity) | Archetype B: Ambon (Archipelagic Hub) |
|---|---|---|
| **Uber H3 Resolution** | High resolution (res 8–9): hexagons are small (~0.1–0.7 km²) to capture granular store density and neighborhood-level canvassing. | Low resolution (res 6–7): hexagons are larger (~4–22 km²) because demand is scattered thinly across vast coastal stretches or distinct villages (*negeri*). |
| **OSM Routing Profile** | Traffic-heavy profile: leverages time-dependent routing matrices in OSRM to simulate morning/evening rush hours and motorbike lane splits. | Topographic/ferry profile: includes slope/elevation data for steep terrain and integrates ferry routes (`route=ferry`) as valid transit links. |
| **VROOM Time Windows** | Soft constraints with buffer: high penalty for tardiness, focus on dense, rapid sequencing. | Hard constraints tied to cut-offs: rigidly tied to immutable constraints like daylight delivery hours, store closing times, or ferry departure schedules. |

## 3. Mathematical Modeling with Regional Modifiers

We introduce a **Regional Complexity Factor** ($\Omega$) to the routing and demand equation so the algorithm does not treat an Ambon kilometer the same as a Jakarta kilometer.

**Hexagon demand weight:**

$$W_h = \Omega_{region} \cdot \left[(\alpha \cdot P_h) + (\beta \cdot O_h) + (\gamma \cdot S_h)\right]$$

**Adapted travel-time matrix** — the matrix $T_{ij}$ generated by OSM is modified by a regional friction coefficient:

$$T_{ij}^{adapted} = T_{ij} \times (1 + \mu_{traffic}) + \delta_{maritime}$$

- **Jakarta:** $\mu_{traffic}$ scales exponentially during rush hours (07:00–09:00, 16:00–19:00), making alternative local-road routing or motorbike dispatch mandatory.
- **Ambon:** $\mu_{traffic}$ is near zero, but $\delta_{maritime}$ (fixed vessel wait times / port processing) acts as a heavy step-function delay if a route requires crossing to Seram or Saparua islands.

## 5. Strategic Field Applications

- **Jakarta canvassing:** the engine prioritizes high-frequency, low-payload motorbike sales visits, packing dozens of small time-window jobs closely together.
- **Ambon canvassing:** the engine handles larger, multi-day loops. It prevents vehicles from stranding on one side of the Leitimur or Hitu peninsula by calculating hard cut-off times dictated by geography and infrastructure.

## 6. The Global Objective Function

Before applying physics, we define exactly what the algorithm is trying to solve. In a hybrid delivery-canvassing network, the goal is not just minimizing distance; it is **maximizing the value of territory covered** while respecting rigid time and capacity constraints.

We define the objective function as minimizing the total cost $Z$:

$$Z = \min \left( \sum_{i \in V} \sum_{j \in V} \sum_{k \in K} C_{ijk} X_{ijk} + \sum_{h \in H} \rho_h (1 - Y_h) \right)$$

Where:

- $V$ — set of all exact delivery nodes (point A to B)
- $K$ — set of available vehicles/sales reps
- $C_{ijk}$ — contextual travel cost (time + fuel) between node $i$ and node $j$ using vehicle $k$
- $X_{ijk}$ — binary variable (1 if vehicle $k$ travels from $i$ to $j$, 0 otherwise)
- $H$ — set of all H3 canvassing hexagons
- $\rho_h$ — calculated opportunity cost (lost potential sales) of *not* visiting hexagon $h$
- $Y_h$ — binary variable (1 if hexagon $h$ is canvassed, 0 otherwise)

This creates a **tension system**: the algorithm wants to minimize travel cost ($C_{ijk}$) but is penalized ($\rho_h$) if it ignores high-value canvassing areas.

## 7. The Astrophysics Injection: Gravity Model of Spatial Demand

If the algorithm evaluated every single store or potential customer in a district to compute $Y_h$, computational complexity would grow exponentially (NP-hard), crashing the engine.

Instead, we apply a **Gravitational Interaction Model**: each H3 hexagon is a celestial body possessing "mass" (demand), and the sales rep/vehicle is an object experiencing gravitational pull.

The attractive force $A_{ij}$ (the priority to move from current location $i$ to canvassing hexagon $j$):

$$A_{ij} = G \left( \frac{M_i^\alpha \cdot M_j^\beta}{T_{ij}^\gamma} \right)$$

Where:

- $G$ — base strategic priority constant (e.g., higher during new product launches)
- $M_j$ — "mass" of the target H3 cell (composite scalar of population density, historical order volume, and store density)
- $M_i$ — remaining capacity or "momentum" of the vehicle at node $i$
- $T_{ij}$ — travel time between $i$ and $j$
- $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ — elasticity parameters; setting $\gamma > 1$ heavily penalizes distant hexagons, tightly clustering the route

**Efficiency gain:** instead of calculating routes to 10,000 individual micro-shops, the algorithm calculates the gravitational pull of ~50 H3 centroids. The vehicle is mathematically pulled toward the clusters with the highest mass-to-distance ratio.

## 8. The Thermodynamics Injection: Simulated Annealing for Route Search

Even with reduced nodes via the gravity model, finding the absolute perfect visit sequence across an asymmetric map like Indonesia is mathematically exhaustive.

To prevent the algorithm from getting stuck in a **local minimum** (e.g., finding an okay route in South Jakarta but missing a vastly superior loop that includes East Jakarta), we use **Simulated Annealing** — a concept derived from the thermodynamics of cooling metals.

A "temperature" variable $T$ starts extremely hot and cools over time. When the system is hot, the algorithm intentionally accepts worse routing choices to broadly explore the map; as it cools, it locks into the most efficient path.

The probability of accepting a worse route (Boltzmann distribution):

$$P(\text{accept}) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } \Delta E < 0 \\ \exp\left(-\dfrac{\Delta E}{k \cdot T}\right) & \text{if } \Delta E \geq 0 \end{cases}$$

Where:

- $\Delta E$ — change in routing cost (time/distance) between the current route and the proposed alternative
- $T$ — current algorithm temperature (decreases over iterations)
- $k$ — scaling constant

**Efficiency gain:** the engine rapidly approximates a near-perfect route across highly disconnected archipelagic hubs (Ambon) or complex gridlocks (Jakarta) without running out of memory.

## 9. The Fluid Dynamics Injection: Topological Viscosity

To handle the Jakarta-vs-Ambon dichotomy purely through math, we apply **kinematic viscosity** ($\nu$) to the road network: the travel medium is not an empty void but a fluid the vehicle must push through.

Effective travel time incorporates the viscosity of the network:

$$T_{ij}^{eff} = \left( \frac{D_{ij}}{v_{base}} \right) \cdot (1 + \nu_{topology}) + \tau_{discrete}$$

Where:

- $\nu_{topology}$ — continuous friction of the environment. In Jakarta this is tied to live traffic density (high viscosity); in rural Maluku it represents road quality or steep elevation changes.
- $\tau_{discrete}$ — a "quantum leap" time penalty: discrete, unavoidable delays like waiting for a roll-on/roll-off (RoRo) ferry schedule. In Jakarta $\tau_{discrete} = 0$; in Ambon it functions as a massive time barrier.

This framework ensures the engine does not blindly draw lines between coordinates, but evaluates the landscape as a **physical environment with gravity, friction, and thermal states**.
